A. Weakness of the Weimar Republic
Created the “problem” → Nazis sold themselves as the “solution”.
1) Political instability Coalition governments made democracy look ineffective.
3 details:
  • Frequent government changes suggested “no one is in charge”.
  • Extremists gained support by promising decisive action.
  • People grew willing to accept less democratic solutions.
2) Economic crises Hyperinflation + Great Depression destroyed trust.
3 details:
  • Mass unemployment pushed voters towards radical parties.
  • Mainstream parties were blamed for failing to deliver stability.
  • Nazi promises of jobs and recovery sounded convincing.
3) Treaty of Versailles resentment Treaty seen as humiliation.
3 details:
  • War guilt + reparations created anger and bitterness.
  • Weimar leaders labelled “November Criminals”.
  • Nationalists backed parties promising to overturn it.
→ Link to outcome
→ Why Nazis rose
Weimar weaknesses produced fear, anger, and desperation. Nazis exploited these conditions by promising stability, jobs, and restored pride—turning crisis into votes.
B. Hitler and Nazi Strengths
How the Nazis converted discontent into real political power.
1) Hitler’s charisma Presented himself as a strong saviour.
3 details:
  • Simple, confident answers during uncertainty.
  • Contrasted sharply with coalition indecision.
  • Mass rallies spread message and loyalty quickly.
2) Reorganisation after 1923 Shifted to legal takeover and grassroots structure.
3 details:
  • Built local party networks across Germany.
  • Centralised leadership under Hitler improved discipline.
  • Propaganda became systematic and targeted.
3) SA intimidation Projected strength + fear.
3 details:
  • Protected Nazi rallies; disrupted opponents’ meetings.
  • Made Nazis look like “order” amid chaos.
  • Reduced opposition space through intimidation.
→ Link to outcome
→ How Nazis gained traction
Nazi methods (propaganda + leadership image + intimidation) turned social anger into mass support, weakening opponents and helping the party become a major electoral force.
C. Great Depression — Catalyst + Evidence
Use elections as evidence: 1928 (fringe) → 1932 (largest party).
1) Economic shock Mass unemployment and insecurity.
3 details:
  • Business failures + wage cuts + poverty.
  • Mainstream parties blamed for helplessness.
  • Extremists offered “radical fix” narratives.
2) Election contrast 1928 → 1932 shows crisis-driven swing.
3 details:
  • 1928: low support during relative stability.
  • 1932: surge as the crisis deepened.
  • Shows depression created a mass audience for Nazism.
3) Not the only factor Nazis needed tools to convert anger into power.
3 details:
  • Charisma and propaganda made promises believable.
  • Organisation mobilised support nationwide.
  • SA intimidation weakened resistance.
→ Link to outcome
→ Judgement link
The Great Depression was the key catalyst, but Nazi strategy and Hitler’s leadership were decisive in turning crisis into sustained political dominance.
D. Road to Dictatorship (1933–1934)
Emergency → law → elimination of rivals → total authority.
1) Reichstag Fire → repression Used as pretext to crush opposition.
3 details:
  • Emergency powers reduced civil liberties.
  • Opponents arrested; fear increased.
  • Opposition weakened before major votes.
2) Enabling Act → rule by decree Hitler could make laws without parliament.
3 details:
  • Parliament sidelined; democracy ended.
  • Legal basis for dismantling remaining checks.
  • Allowed rapid transformation into a totalitarian state.
3) Night of the Long Knives + Army oath Secured military loyalty and removed threats.
3 details:
  • Purged SA leadership and enemies, removing internal rivals.
  • Army reassured and more willing to support Hitler.
  • Personal oath reduced chances of military resistance.
→ Link to outcome
→ Why dictatorship succeeded
Hitler combined legal authority (Enabling Act) with fear (Fire repression) and force (purges + army loyalty), removing opposition and checks to become sole ruler by 1934.
